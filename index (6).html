Christopher O'Reilly

Founder, Liberty Soup Run · The Liberties, Dublin 8

Chris O'Reilly grew up in The Liberties and experienced addiction firsthand. After his own recovery, he began bringing food and support to people on the same streets he once walked himself — starting with a single table and a flask of hot water on Meath Street.

What follows on this page is the organisation's complete story: where it started, exactly how the nightly route works, what it provides beyond food, and every way you can help it keep going.

01Origins

Founded by someone who had lived it.

Like many in the area, Chris experienced addiction firsthand, and spent years finding his way through it before coming out the other side. Once he found his own recovery, he didn't walk away from the streets that had shaped him — he went back to them, this time with a flask of hot water and a few sandwiches, set up on a small table on Meath Street.

There was no grant, no committee, and no five-year plan. Just one man who understood, better than most, what it meant to need someone to show up. Word travelled the way it does in a tight community — neighbour to neighbour, then further. People who'd been helped came back to help others.

"It's just about giving people a chance to get their lives back."

— Chris O'Reilly, Founder

📍

The Liberties, Dublin 8 — one of Dublin's oldest working-class neighbourhoods, traditionally associated with markets, terraced housing and tight-knit community. Liberty Soup Run is rooted in, and run by, that same community.

How It Grew

From one table to a nightly operation.

Then

  • One small table on Meath Street
  • A flask of hot water and a few sandwiches
  • One founder, working alone
  • No funding of any kind

Now

  • Two vans covering set routes every night
  • Walking outreach teams with trolleys
  • 33 volunteers and growing
  • Still no government funding — donor-funded, entirely

What We Believe

The principles behind every night out.

Dignity, always

Everyone who comes to a van or a walking team is treated as a person first — no forms, no conditions, no judgement.

Volunteer-led

There is no paid staff. Every hour given is given freely, by people from the area who choose to show up, night after night.

Community rooted

This started in The Liberties, for The Liberties. Local knowledge shapes every route — which streets, which corners, which faces.

Independent

No government funding means no strings attached — and a direct line between every donation and the people it reaches.

03Essentials

More than a hot meal.

Every van and walking round carries more than soup — because a night on the street takes more than food to survive.

Food & Hot Drinks

Hot soup, sandwiches, tea and coffee — prepared fresh and served every night, seven nights a week.

Clothing & Toiletries

Donated clothing, hygiene products, and basic essentials, sorted and distributed where they're needed most.

Sleeping Bags & Tents

Warmth and shelter for people sleeping rough — handed out directly to those who need them, no questions asked.

A Friendly Face

For many people on the route, a few minutes of conversation matters as much as anything handed across the van door.

Beyond the Van

Support that doesn't end at the street corner.

Liberty Soup Run's work doesn't stop at handing out food. Volunteers help connect people to treatment services when they're ready to take that step — and the organisation offers practical, ongoing support to families working to transition out of homelessness toward stability.

None of this happens through forms or referral systems. It happens because the same volunteers show up, night after night, and people come to trust a familiar face.

04Vision

The café is coming. Help us get there.

The nightly run reaches people in the moment — a hot meal, a sleeping bag, a few minutes of company on a cold street. But moments aren't enough to rebuild a life. People need somewhere steady: a room that's warm, a chair that's theirs for as long as they want it, and a door that's open outside of the hours the vans are out.

That's the idea behind the café. Not a shelter, and not a soup kitchen with four walls — a genuine community space in The Liberties where the same people the vans meet at night can sit down, eat a proper meal, and be treated like the regulars they are.

The Bigger Goal

The café is the first step, not the whole plan. After losing people on the streets in recent years, the team has been open about needing more than a nightly run can offer on its own — more access to residential treatment, and more drop-in spaces people can walk into during the day, not just at night. The café is where that next chapter starts.

Questions About the Campaign

Will the nightly soup run continue once the café opens?

+

Yes. The café is an addition to the nightly outreach service, not a replacement for it. The vans and walking teams will keep running seven nights a week regardless of how the campaign progresses.

How will my donation be used?

+

Donations to the café fund go toward securing and fitting out a space in The Liberties. As an entirely volunteer-led, donor-funded organisation, every contribution counts directly toward that goal.

When will the café open?

+

There's no fixed date yet — the timeline depends on funding and finding the right space. Supporters who get in touch will be the first to know as the campaign progresses.

06Recognition

Noticed by the people who see the work up close.

Liberty Soup Run has been recognised by Dublin City Council and An Garda Síochána for its outreach work in the city centre, and has been covered by The Liberty, the local Dublin 8 newspaper, for its presence on the streets of the area.

In December 2023, UFC fighter Conor McGregor stopped to meet the team in person, donated €10,000 on the spot, and later helped the organisation source a new van. Chris O'Reilly described the moment as "mind blowing." — Newstalk, Dec 2023
